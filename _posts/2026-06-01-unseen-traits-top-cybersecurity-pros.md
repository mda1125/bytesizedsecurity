What Makes Someone Good at Cybersecurity Has Nothing to Do With Hacking

TL;DR: A Reddit thread asked cybersecurity professionals what personality traits matter most. The answers weren't technical skills. They were curiosity, patience, humility, and empathy.

---

I've worked with brilliant security engineers who couldn't keep a job. I've also worked with average-skilled people who became the most trusted person on every team.

The difference was never technical ability. It was personality.

A thread on r/cybersecurity asked what traits matter most. Hundreds of comments rolled in.

## 1. Curiosity

The runaway winner. The best security professionals pull on threads. They see something off in a log and follow it. They read a CVE advisory, then go read the patch diff. They don't stop at "it works." They need to know why.

## 2. Patience

One commenter put it well: "Even if I have to say the same thing 5 times, if I treat each time like the first time, my blood won't boil."

A huge part of this job is explaining risks to non-technical people. When someone clicks the phishing link after your third training, you still need patience for the conversation afterward.

## 3. Humility

"The absolute WORST trait in cybersecurity is ego," one commenter wrote. "The people everyone wants to work with admit what they don't know."

This field moves too fast for anyone to know everything. If your identity is wrapped up in being the smartest person in the room, you'll either fake it (dangerous) or stop learning (worse).

## 4. Calm Under Pressure

Incident response at 2 AM. A breach with executives demanding answers. A zero-day on a Friday with no patch.

Your ability to stay focused when everything is on fire matters more than any certification. You don't need to be emotionless. You need to be steady.

## 5. Empathy

This kept surfacing independently: "Too many security professionals without empathy." "Security folk tends to be condescending." "Grace and kindness is lacking."

If people are afraid to report mistakes because the security team will humiliate them, incidents go unreported. One commenter nailed it: "Having people like to work with you is the biggest superpower."

## My Take

After 8+ years, I agree. The best people to work with are curious, patient, honest about their limits, calm when things break, and kind.

Technical skills are trainable. These traits aren't.

Ask yourself: "Am I curious enough to keep learning, patient enough to explain things twice, and honest enough to say when I don't know?" If yes, you'll do fine.
