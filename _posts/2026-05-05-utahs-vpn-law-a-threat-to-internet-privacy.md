Utah's New VPN Law Is a Warning Sign for Internet Privacy Everywhere

TL;DR: Starting May 6, 2026, Utah becomes the first U.S. state to target VPN usage in law. Senate Bill 73 holds websites liable for verifying the age of anyone physically in the state, regardless of VPN use, and bans platforms from sharing information about VPNs. The law is technically unenforceable, raises First Amendment concerns, and sets a dangerous precedent for digital privacy rights.

---

## What Happened

Utah Governor Spencer Cox signed Senate Bill 73 ("Online Age Verification Amendments") on March 19, 2026. While most of the bill deals with a 2% tax on online adult content revenue (effective October), one section goes into effect this Wednesday, May 6: provisions that directly regulate VPN access.

This makes Utah the first state in the nation to target VPN use as a means to bypass age-verification gates.

## What the Law Does

SB 73 addresses VPN use in two ways:

**1. Location-based liability regardless of VPN use**

Under the new law, if you are physically in Utah, you are considered to be accessing a website from Utah. Period. It doesn't matter if you're using a VPN, proxy server, or any other tool to mask your location.

Websites hosting content deemed "harmful to minors" are responsible for verifying the age of anyone in Utah, even if they have no way of knowing the user is in Utah.

**2. A ban on sharing VPN information**

Commercial entities hosting "a substantial portion of material harmful to minors" are prohibited from:
- Facilitating or encouraging VPN use to bypass age checks
- Providing instructions on how to use a VPN
- Providing the means to circumvent geofencing

## Why This Is a Problem

**The liability trap**

If a website is legally required to verify the age of all users physically in Utah, but it has no reliable way to determine who is using a VPN from Utah, the only "safe" options are:
- Block all known VPN IP addresses (punishing millions of legitimate VPN users worldwide)
- Mandate age verification for every visitor globally

Both options are terrible for users.

**First Amendment concerns**

Barring platforms from sharing truthful information about legal privacy tools is a speech restriction. VPNs are legal. Explaining how they work is legal. This law changes that for certain platforms in Utah.

**Technical impossibility**

Blocking all VPN traffic is a game that no company wins. VPN providers add new IP addresses constantly. No comprehensive blocklist exists. If commercial VPNs get restricted, users will move to:
- Private tunnels through cloud services (AWS, Azure, GCP)
- Non-commercial proxies
- Residential proxies that look identical to standard home internet traffic

These alternatives emerge within hours of any new restriction.

## Who Gets Hurt

This law won't stop a determined teenager with basic technical knowledge. It will impact:
- Journalists who rely on VPNs to protect sources
- Abuse survivors who use VPNs to prevent stalkers from tracking their location
- Remote workers who use VPNs for corporate security
- Regular residents who use VPNs to keep their browsing data away from advertisers and data brokers

## The Bigger Picture

Utah's approach follows a predictable cycle: governments pass age-verification mandates, VPN usage spikes in response, and instead of reconsidering the failed approach, lawmakers target the privacy tools themselves.

This isn't staying within Utah's borders. The UK Children's Commissioner has called VPNs a "loophole that needs closing." France's Minister for AI and Digital Affairs has said VPNs are "the next topic on my list." The precedent Utah sets here gives ammunition to regulators worldwide who view privacy tools as obstacles rather than essential infrastructure.

As the EFF puts it: "Lawmakers who can't distinguish between a security tool and a 'loophole' are now writing the rules for one of the most complex infrastructures on Earth."

## What You Should Know

- VPNs remain legal to use in Utah. The law does not ban individuals from using them.
- The law targets websites, not users directly. It creates liability for platforms, not for people running VPN software.
- The "don't ask, don't tell" enforcement style means websites likely only have an obligation to verify age if they learn a user is in Utah and using a VPN. If they don't know, their obligation remains unclear.
- This is the first law of its kind in the U.S. Wisconsin proposed something similar but removed the VPN provisions after pushback. Utah did not.
